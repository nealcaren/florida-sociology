What Florida Changed in Your Sociology Textbook

A comparison of the original OpenStax Introduction to Sociology 3e with Florida's state-modified version.

When sociologists apply the sociological perspective and begin to ask questions, almost no topic is off limits.

One word — inserted by Florida's editors — hedges what sociology is allowed to study.

That was one change. There were 291.

Florida adopted a modified version of the widely-used OpenStax Introduction to Sociology textbook — then cut it nearly in half. Entire chapters on race, gender, sexuality, and economic inequality were removed. What remained was systematically edited: names deleted, data softened, critical frameworks replaced with vague gestures toward "more advanced courses."

The changes fall into four patterns.

Each of these changes is small enough to miss on its own. Taken together, they amount to a different textbook — one that avoids naming racism, erases LGBTQ people, softens inequality data, and removes the theoretical tools students would use to analyze power. The original is an introduction to sociology. The Florida version is an introduction to what's left when the discipline's most consequential questions have been set aside.
